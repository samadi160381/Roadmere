Three Days on Highway 12, Utah

As told by a Roadmere traveler

I'd been warned that Highway 12 was scenic. What nobody mentioned was how the road itself seems to know it — how it curves along ridgelines with no guardrail between you and a few hundred feet of open air, like it wants you to feel small on purpose.

We started in Bryce Canyon before sunrise, mostly because someone in a gas station the night before told us the hoodoos look like they're on fire at first light. They weren't exaggerating. The rock spires catch the sun before anything else does, and for about ten minutes the whole canyon turns the color of embers.

From there, Highway 12 climbs out of Bryce and does something I wasn't ready for — it puts you on a ridge called the Hogback, a stretch where the road narrows and drops away on both sides into canyons hundreds of feet deep. There's nowhere to safely stop and take it in. You just drive it, hands a little tighter on the wheel, trying to watch the road and the view at the same time.

"You just drive it, hands a little tighter on the wheel, trying to watch the road and the view at the same time."

We stopped for the night in Boulder, Utah — a town so small it barely interrupts the drive, but the kind of place where the one restaurant serves food good enough to make you forget you're in the middle of nowhere. The stars that night were the kind you only get a few hours from the nearest city with real light pollution. No phone signal, no reason to check one anyway.

Day two took us through Capitol Reef, where the road drops into a canyon lined with old fruit orchards planted by Mormon settlers over a century ago. You can pick fruit in season, straight off public trees, which felt like a strange and generous detail for a place this remote.

By day three, the canyons had opened up into wider desert, and the drive slowed down in a different way — less adrenaline, more just watching the landscape shift color as the afternoon went on. We ended in Torrey, tired in the good way, already talking about which stretch we'd drive slower next time.

If you're planning this route: give yourself more time than you think you need. Every overlook we planned to skip ended up being the one we stopped at longest.
