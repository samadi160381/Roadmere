The Slow Way Through the Ozarks

3 days on the road 190 miles covered Arkansas Ozark Highlands

The map from the gas station in Mountain View was drawn in ballpoint pen on the back of a receipt, and it turned out to be more accurate than the GPS. "Once you lose signal past the Buffalo River, just follow the ridge," the man behind the counter said, and handed it over like he'd done it a hundred times before. He probably had.

This is not a highway route. There's no Highway 1 sunset moment, no single photo that sums it up. The Ozarks reward a different kind of attention — the plan is to go slow, take the county roads that don't show up on the interstate atlas, and let the towns of a few hundred people set the pace.

01 — Day OneOff the interstate at Mountain View

Most people pass through Mountain View for the folk music — the courthouse square fills with pickers most evenings, unplanned and unpaid, just neighbors trading fiddle tunes until dark. It's worth pulling over for an hour before continuing on, even if the plan is to keep moving. The gas station map started here for a reason: this is the last town with a stoplight for a while.

From Mountain View, Arkansas Highway 5 climbs and drops through hardwood forest that hasn't been logged in a generation. The road follows the contour of the land instead of cutting through it, which means more curves and slower miles, but also means every third turn opens onto a valley view worth the extra twenty minutes.

"You don't drive the Ozarks so much as you follow them — the roads were laid down by water first, and the asphalt just came later."

02 — Day TwoCreek roads and the Buffalo River

The Buffalo National River is the spine of this route, and the gravel roads that shadow it are where the hand-drawn map earns its keep. Turn off the highway at Jasper and the pavement disappears within a mile — low-water crossings, switchbacks with no guardrail, and views of the river bluffs that no overlook parking lot can match because there isn't one. Slow down. This is not a road for making time.

Cell service comes and goes out here, which is exactly the point of the paper map. Gas up in Jasper before heading out — the next reliable pump is nearly forty miles on, and the kind of forty miles that takes two hours.

03 — Day ThreeEureka Springs and the road back

The route closes the loop in Eureka Springs, a town built into a hillside so steep that streets stack on top of each other and addresses list a floor number instead of a direction. After two days of empty roads, it feels almost like arriving somewhere. Stay the night if the schedule allows — the town looks different lit up after dark than it does from a car window at noon.

The last stretch back toward the interstate follows Highway 23, nicknamed the "Pig Trail" for reasons that become obvious after the first dozen switchbacks. It's the closest thing this route has to a signature drive: tight curves through oak forest, climbing hard for a few miles before dropping just as fast into the valley on the other side. Take it slow — not because you have to, but because there's no reason not to.
