Chasing Sunset on Highway 1

1 day, planned as a single stretch 90 miles of coastline Big Sur, California

The plan was simple: leave Monterey by nine, hit four photo stops, and be in Big Sur proper for a golden-hour dinner. The plan lasted about forty-five minutes, right up until the first turnout past Bixby Bridge, where a whale surfaced offshore and every car on the road pulled over at once. Eleven stops later, dinner happened well after dark, and it was worth every minute of the delay.

Highway 1 through Big Sur is only about ninety miles, and most guides suggest you could drive it in under two hours. Nobody who has actually driven it agrees. The road hugs cliffs three hundred feet above the water, and it rewards the kind of driving where the destination stops mattering somewhere around mile twenty.

01 — MorningBixby Bridge and the first detour

Bixby Creek Bridge shows up on every California postcard for a reason, but arriving before nine means having it almost to yourself. The turnout on the north side gets crowded by ten; the smaller pullout on the south side, a quarter mile further, rarely does. This is where the day's schedule quietly fell apart — a pod of gray whales moving south, close enough to see the spray, and no one in a hurry to leave.

"You don't really choose your stops on Highway 1. The road decides for you, one turnout at a time."

02 — MiddayMcWay Falls and lunch in the redwoods

Julia Pfeiffer Burns State Park is the reason most people plan this drive in the first place: an eighty-foot waterfall that drops directly onto the beach below, visible from a short half-mile trail. It gets busy by noon, but the crowd thins fast if you're willing to walk the extra ten minutes north along the coastal trail, where the same view opens up with nobody else around.

03 — EveningThe stretch nobody plans for

South of Nepenthe, the crowds thin out fast, and the last twenty miles into the golden hour felt like a different road entirely. This is where the eleventh stop happened — no name, no sign, just a wide shoulder where the cliff dropped away and the whole coast turned orange and gold for about fifteen minutes. It's not on any list of official viewpoints. It didn't need to be.

Dinner ended up being a gas station sandwich eaten on the hood of the car, well after dark, watching the last color drain out of the sky over the water. Not the reservation that had been planned three weeks earlier. Better than it.
